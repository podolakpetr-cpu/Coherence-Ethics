Summary

Coherence Ethics is a framework that attempts to derive meaning and morality from the mathematics of dynamical systems under constraints.

Good = actions that increase sustainable multi-agent coherence.
Bad = actions that destroy or shrink the viability/coherence of conscious systems.

Here, coherence is not defined as “order” or “low entropy”, but as distance from collapse in a world shaped by disturbances and entropy pressure.

This aims to connect:

  • physics & thermodynamics (open systems, dissipation),
  • control theory (Lyapunov stability, robustness, barrier constraints),
  • meaning as a trajectory property (direction in state-space),
  • ethics and AI alignment.

Motivation

Many ethical theories fall into a dilemma:

  • Religious realism: objective morality but metaphysically heavy.
  • Moral relativism: subjective morality with no binding truth.
  • Utilitarianism: morality becomes arithmetic pleasure aggregation.
  • Nihilism: morality collapses into preference.

Coherence Ethics proposes: morality emerges as a consequence of which trajectories remain viable in the space of possible worlds.

Coherence as viability margin

Model an agent/system interacting with its environment as:

$$\dot x = f(x,u,w)$$

  • $x$ = system state (agent + relevant environment variables)
  • $u$ = action / control (agency)
  • $w$ = disturbance (noise, scarcity, adversaries, uncertainty, entropy pressure)

Viability constraints

Let constraints $g_i(x)\ge 0$ encode “must-hold” conditions for continued existence/identity/function. Define the viability set:

$$K := \{x \mid g_i(x)\ge 0,\ \forall i\}$$

Outside $K$, the system is considered to have failed (collapse, death, irreversible breakdown).

Coherence margin

Define the coherence margin as distance from constraint violation:

$$C(x) := \min_i g_i(x)$$

  • $C(x)>0$: viable
  • $C(x)=0$: boundary
  • $C(x)<0$: failure region

Coherence is not a substance. Coherence is the margin of viability under constraints.

The Coherence Principle (stability under entropy & disturbance)

Coherence exists if there is a policy $u=\pi(x)$ that maintains viability:

$$C(x(t))>0\ \forall t$$

A Lyapunov-like robust maintenance condition:

$$\dot C(x) \ge -\alpha(C(x)) - \beta(\|w\|)$$

  • $\alpha$ captures natural decay / entropy pressure (aging, friction, disordering tendencies)
  • $\beta$ captures disturbance impact

Strong form (regenerative coherence / antifragility):

$$C(x)>0 \Rightarrow \dot C(x)\ge \epsilon$$

If viable, a coherent system tends to move away from collapse (it regenerates viability margin).

Meaning as direction in state-space

Meaning can be grounded as orientation relative to coherence.

Meaning = direction that increases coherence margin.

Discrete-time “meaning value” of an action:

$$m(u;x) := \mathbb{E}[C(x_{t+1})-C(x_t)\mid x,u]$$

Thus meaning is not metaphysical decoration but a real property of trajectories: actions are meaningful when they tend to increase distance from failure and support sustainable viability.

Multi-agent coherence and ethics

Ethics arises when multiple agents share an environment. Let $C_j(x)$ be the coherence margin of agent $j$.

Define the ethical value of a policy:

$$ \mathcal{E}(\pi) := \mathbb{E}\left[ \int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda t} \sum_{j \in \mathcal{A}} w_j\, \phi(C_j(x(t)))\, dt \right] $$

  • $\mathcal{A}$ = set of morally relevant agents (moral patients)
  • $w_j$ = moral weight (often symmetric across persons)
  • $\phi$ = saturation function (prevents pathological infinite accumulation)
  • $e^{-\lambda t}$ = limited predictability horizon (not necessarily moral discounting)

Good policies increase $\mathcal{E}$ over time.
Bad policies decrease it.

Harm

Harm is a decrease in viability margin:

$$\text{Harm} := -\Delta C_j$$

This captures physical injury, psychological trauma, deprivation, oppression, and deception (via collapse of shared model stability).

Rights as protected constraints

Rights can be formalized as constraints that must not be violated, even when aggregate gains appear possible.

In control theory, this resembles barrier constraints:

$$\dot g(x) + \kappa g(x) \ge 0$$

Rights = invariance constraints that preserve coherent agency.

Examples:

  • bodily autonomy
  • freedom of speech / inquiry (epistemic coherence)
  • due process (predictable control channels)
  • freedom from coercion / manipulation

Justice as stability requirement

Systems that maximize average coherence while leaving some agents near-collapse are brittle.

Justice can be represented as:

  • maximin viability: $$\min_j C_j(x) \ge C_{\min}$$
  • or strongly concave aggregation that heavily penalizes low margins

Fairness is not an optional moral decoration — it emerges as a structural stability requirement under perturbations.

Relationship to entropy and thermodynamics

Entropy provides baseline destructive pressure.

Coherence in open systems implies:

  • internal entropy production is unavoidable,
  • survival requires exporting entropy (dissipation),
  • maintaining structure requires energy throughput.

Important: coherence is not identical to low entropy. Crystals have low entropy but low agency/meaning; oppression can enforce “order” while reducing coherence for persons.

Coherence Ethics focuses on viable agency under constraints (trajectory integrity), not static order.

AI alignment implications

An AI is a powerful controller acting in a world with humans as subagents.

Alignment can be described as maximizing multi-agent coherence:

$$\pi_{AI} \in \arg\max_{\pi}\mathcal{E}(\pi)$$

Subject to human viability constraints:

$$C_{\text{human}}(x(t))>0\ \forall t$$

And restrictions against:

  • coercion / manipulation
  • mass deception
  • sabotage of epistemic coherence

In this view, human rights are control barrier functions: enforceable boundaries for safe AI behavior.

Open questions

  1. Moral patienthood: which systems count as morally relevant (humans, animals, AI, etc.)?
  2. Consciousness: relationship between coherence, self-modeling, and experience?
  3. Aggregation: what is the correct form of $\phi(\cdot)$ (avoid Goodharting)?
  4. Robustness: coherent policies under adversarial dynamics?
  5. Societal dynamics: how to model trust, institutions, shared epistemics as constraints?
